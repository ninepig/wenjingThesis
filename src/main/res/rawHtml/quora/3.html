Yes and no. Though China ranked 2rd in total GDP, in terms of GDP per capita, China ranked 73th while USA ranked 6th and UK ranked 13th. Right, China is relatively poor, but not “very” poor. On account of the fact that China is a upper middle income country by world standard.

List of countries by GDP (nominal) per capita - Wikipedia

** ** ** ** ** ** ** ** ** ** ** **

Reading some of the answers in this question, I saw many common misunderstandings.

For example:

*The western part of China is undeveloped and poor, many of us have only experienced life in coastal provinces rather than the whole China.

Actually, most Chinese people live in Eastern Coastal provinces & municipality such as, Shandong, Zhejiang, Jiangsu, Shanghai, Fujian and Guangdong/Canton and in many other Central provinces. These six coastal provinces mentioned above have a total population of about 400 million, make up 30% of total population of China.

Obviously not a few Chinese enjoy comparatively good life.

Most Chinese (96% of total population) live on the land below that line. The name of that line is Hu Line.

“自黑龙江之瑷珲向西南作一直线，至云南之腾冲为止，分全国为东南与西北两部：则此东南部之面积，计四百万平方千米，约占全国总面积之百分之三十六；西北部之面积，计七百万方公里，约占全国总面积之百分之六十四。惟人口之分布，则东南部计四万万四千万，约占总人口之百分之九十六；西北部之人口，仅一千八百万，约占全国总人口之百分之四。”

—胡焕庸，《中国人口之分布》

Therefore, it’s inappropriate to regard living conditions in western China as representative life of ordinary Chinese people.

(China is) Worse off than some, better off than many.That’s my conclusion.

Q. Is China very poor?

Short answer:

  • No, by GDP PPP. An advanced economic power.
  • Yes, by GDP PPP Per Capita (per population). A developing country.

(1) China is among the economic superpowers by the metric of GDP PPP ( List of countries by GDP (PPP)): #1

(2) China is ranked a lowly #84 by GDP (PPP) per capita because of its massive population base. Yes, poor, by this metric.

(3) Economic powers typically reach top-band in both metrics in one swoop. China has this economic ‘dual personality’ which befuddles everyone, often, including the Chinese themselves.

Rick Li, Strategy & Change
Written Mar 13

Whether we define a country as poor or rich will depend on the statistical indicators used to qualify it. Moreover, if we chart out the people’s wealth of any country, it will probably be within the ‘Bell Curve’. There will be some that are at the extreme Rich and those at the extreme Poor levels. Poverty and poverty level is also relative to every country.

According to World Bank data, for China as a country, the 2015 GDP (at PPP level) of China is at $19.8 trillion, while that of the United States is at $18 trillion - Here. This means that using the indicator GDP (at PPP level), China is in fact richer that the United States. Using the same indicator, but on ‘per capita’ basis, China GDP per capita will then be about $14,100, while that of the United States will be about $56,250. This means on a per capita basis, each American citizen is in fact 4 times richer than the Chinese counterpart.

In fact, the World Bank is now classifying countries according to Gross National Income (GNI) per capita - Here. According to this indicator, China’s GNI per capital is $7,930, while that of the United States is $55,980. Using this indicator, people in the US are in fact 7 times richer than those in China. According to World Bank country categorisation, the United States is in the ‘High Income’ country group, where are China is in the ‘Upper Middle Income’ group. Rick Li's answer to Is China a developing country?

However, there is poverty in the United States. In fact, for 2015, about 13.5% of the America (tantamount to 43.1 million people) are considered to be ‘poor’ - Here. For America, the poverty threshold level (for a family of 2 adults and 2 children) is considered to be $24,339 - Here and Here. But this poverty threshold level is in fact 3 times higher than the GNI per capita in China.

For China, according to Statista, in 2014, the average annual salary of an employee in the urban cities of China ranges from 102,268 yuan (about $16,000) in Beijing to 49,882 yuan (about $8,000) in Hainan - refer to the chart below. But we should also know that in China, there are many in rural areas, where income may be drastically lower than those in urban areas.

If we focus mainly on the major Chinese cities, according to Bloomberg, there are at least 12 cities, where the GDP is more than a trillion yuan (about $150 billion) in 2016. The city with the largest GDP is Shanghai with about 2.7 trillion yuan (about $400 billion).

This also means that the economy of Shanghai city is big enough to be equivalent to the GDP of about 50 of the world smallest economies. But compared to New York city, Shanghai will be still about 4 times smaller.

Therefore, whether China is poor or not is all a matter of relativity. According to some indicators, China seems to be rich, while others seem to portray that China is still behind and has lots to catch up.

Wang Wei-Ming, Just a random guy
Written Dec 13

A proper clarification of the word “poor” might be useful in answering this question. Let’s use World Bank’s definition of poverty. Poverty according to World Bank is anything below PPP of 1.90 USD per day. In that sense, 1.9 percent of the Chinese population or approximately 25 million Chinese live under poverty <Poverty & Equity Data | China | The World Bank >.

However, the Chinese government has its own standard of poverty, 2300 RMB per year, which is slightly above that of the World Bank’s. Using the national standard of poverty, CPAD (China Poverty Alleviation and Development office) states there are approximately 55.75 million people in poverty as of 2016 <《中国的减贫行动与人权进步》白皮书（全文）>.

Over all, China is a big country with a diverse population. It is extremely difficult to say definitively if China is poor or not. But I hope the aforementioned data is helpful to you in some way.
