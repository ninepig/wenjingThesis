Christian Bonilla, Author of "Smart Like How: The Hidden Side of Career Success"
Written Dec 22, 2015

One of the things that I've noticed throughout college and adulthood is how limited the perspectives were among the "smartest" people I knew as far as possible ways to make a good living.  The majority of my friends from college work in one of five industries:

  1. High Finance
  2. Software
  3. Law
  4. Medicine
  5. Consulting

After that, there are a bunch of long-tail professions my friends fall into. Sure, those are big, big industries that attract loads of bright people who collaborate and compete with one another. But think about how many things don't fall under any of those. Look around the room that you're sitting in right now and ask where all of the things in it came from. Most of them were probably manufactured somewhere, which means that  they had to be made, marketed, shipped, and sold by lots of people you've probably never thought about before. In that supply chain there un-sexy businesses that nonetheless make big money doing what they do. Someone has to run those companies that all of the bankers and app developers shun ;)

I return to this question often at Smart Like How, although I look at the problem a bit differently. For me, the question most of us encounter more often is how to stand out among a group of smart, hard-working people when you aren't the hands-down smartest person in the room.  Most of the time, young professionals in this situation respond by attempting to outwork each other and push their limits of exhaustion trying to out-compete all of the other smart people. I call this the Hard Work Paradox, because the smartest, hardest-working people are actually the least able to differentiate themselves on the basis of hard work. And besides, just working hard doesn't guarantee that your output is more innovative or accurate than someone else's - especially if that less-than-intelligent person in your eyes sees the problem differently than you do.

I'd highly recommend that anyone interested in the topic check out the following articles on how to succeed despite not being the absolute smartest person out there. Even if you think you are, you'll benefit from understanding how to translate IQ into business value:

  • Four Things You Won't Hear During Orientation
  • The Hard Work Paradox (Part 2): What I Learned by Watching Other People Succeed
  • Want to Build Career Momentum? Start By Figuring Out What Your Job Really Is
  • Every Business Has Hidden Problems (and That’s a Good Thing)
  • Building Momentum: Become an Authority on Something Valuable
  • Be Careful What You Get Good At
  • When Success Becomes the Enemy: The Promotion Snowball Effect
  • Not a Data Scientist? You Can Still Be Data Savvy
  • How to Drive Adoption of Your Ideas
  • The Seven Types of Failed Thought Leaders

Henry Foster, B.A. Business, St. Anselm College (2017)
Updated Oct 12, 2015
The problem is, people actually believe that they aren't intelligent.

Society has set up limitations and regulations establishing what we consider smart.  We decide that the definition of intelligence is GPA, booksmarts, wittiness, a prestigious college degree, a high office position, etc.

We must understand that there are thousands of ways of being intelligent.

We may not be the fastest readers or be able to code websites, we may not be able to run offices or start a business, but if we can socialize with people well, if we can work hard and honest, if we can find our forte, there's an opportunity for success.

So just as John Geare says, if you're good at moving rocks, find the best way to make money from it.

Alexander Tee
Updated Dec 24, 2015

Nick Granny wrote a wonderful answer but I would like to add some point of my own. Two reasons which I believe is due to the simple principle of supply & demands and Social attractiveness of the job.

Nowadays, "intelligent" people are over-supplied and there are limited amount of jobs for these people. When supply is more than demand, the "price (a.k.a salary)" drops.

At the same time, these very same people stay away from jobs that are perceived as socially unattractive (e.g.: subway driver, bus driver, sewerage cleaner). Thus, decreasing the supply for socially unattractive jobs.

Because of these reasons, many "less intelligent" people pick up the works that no one wants to do and get paid more. Which causes situation like these happen:

  • Factory workers in China can earn up 2 to 3 times the salary of normal white-collar worker.
  • UK's underground tube drivers have better paid than a teachers & nurses

To end my answer, there is this particular worker in a printing shop that I used to order my printing material from. He get paid 4 times higher than a fresh graduate in my company despite not having a degree.

The reason? No one else is willing to work until 2am to 3am (not everyday) as a delivery boy.

Richard Garand, Studying reality to prepare for the test
Written Jan 23, 2016

If you change the question, the answer becomes clear. For example if you want to be in the top 5% of incomes, you need to do something better than 95% of people. If you want to be in the top 0.01% of incomes, you need to do something better than 99.99% of people (like rock stars / sports stars). It's that simple.

Other than being intelligent (which is not that useful for earning an income unless you apply it in the right way), what are some other things you could do better than 95% of people?

It's not that hard. It does take work but you don't need to be born with a special talent. Most people will just go along with what's happening and not try to change it. All it takes is a decision and an effort, and you can be the exception. Here are a few possible options:

  • Be reliable -- keep all your promises
  • Understand the deeper needs that are the real reason you were asked to do something, and come up with other ways to meet those needs
  • See things through instead of leaving when "your time is up"
  • Think really hard about what the people that you work for want, and how to do it for them, instead of always thinking about what you want
  • Take the time to learn new things that could help you and the people around you
  • Volunteer to do the tasks that are hard but necessary, without hesitation
  • Look at examples of people in different situations, and how their decisions or actions might be applicable to what you're doing
  • Be honest and tell people what they need to hear, even if it's hard for them and for you
  • Learn how to communicate with others so they can understand what's going on around them
  • Look at problems around you and think of ways to turn them into opportunities that will make everyone better off
  • Get to know what's important to other people and show them an easier way to get it

Pick any one of those (even better, pick a few). Look around at how everyone else does it, and then decide that you will play at a higher level. Do that for a few years and you will find that great things are happening for you no matter what your intelligence level is.

In fact if you have an IQ of 200 and you don't commit to doing any of these at a higher level than the average person, there's a good chance that you won't get very far.

Intelligence helps with a lot of these but they can all be done with nothing more than a choice and an effort. All of them are hard to do if you don't make the effort. That's why it can take so little to be exceptional.

Well, they go into dot.com or become politicians. If they're not smart enough to become teachers, cooks, police officers, or nurses, they always have the option to become a Scrum Master or get a Six Sigma Black Belt.

I kid. But I have serious issues with your question. "Less than intelligent" is quite the accusation. Reality is, that privilege, gender, age, upbringing, economic and academic opportunities, and more determine where someone winds up. As a baseline we all have the foundation to become the next super-Academic or window cleaner, all it takes is a conspiracy of opportunities and events to turn us one way or another.

Merriam-Webster defines as "intelligence"

[...] the ability to learn or understand or to deal with new or trying situations :  reason; also  :  the skilled use of reason (2)  :  the ability to apply knowledge to manipulate one's environment or to think abstractly as measured by objective criteria (as tests)

I have seen grown men and women break down and cry in situations my cooks could handle without breaking a sweat. Spend a week reading random Tumblr blogs and you'll notice quite the swath of supposedly "intelligent" men and women who can't even deal with old and rather non-trying situations.

Conversely, the single mother working my front of the house deals with her boyfriend (and father of her kids) having been arrested for drug selling, their car being seized, house being foreclosed, mother needing 'round the clock medical care, and many more new and trying situations quite beautifully.

The skilled use of reason: given the large number of academics and STEM technologists believing in invisible men in the sky, fad diets, scare screeds about food stuffs, and craptastic social concepts, I am not so sure they fit that declaration of "intelligence." On the other hand, I've seen ex-cons apply  highly complex reason chains to get their parole officer to let them go to Disneyland with their kids or work in a non-dry kitchen.

Finally, the "ability to apply knowledge to manipulate one's environment" would bring up all those ex-cons (and their time inside), every single one of my cooks, down to the dishwasher and the aforementioned single mother. A lack of wide and deep knowledge doesn't stop them from being forces of nature in the "applying" department, so (according to the above definition) I'd consider them more intelligent than someone who has deeper and wider knowledge but couldn't last a day in their shoes.

Asad Chishty, No impulse control.
Written Nov 25, 2015
I think intelligence is more a matter of experience and perception than something exclusive.

But, with that being said, to answer your question in this context, I'd have to say that less than intelligent people often do better in business than those who are more intelligent. If you have a high level of intellect, you tend to over analyze and think things through over and over again and as a result you make slow, if any, progress. But someone that you would deem less-than-intelligent, won't have all those thoughts because they're not on the same thought plane. So instead of asking about what can go wrong or how they need to assess the situation before jumping in, they simple jump in and adjust to the situation as it evolves, and more often than not, they end up being successful faster because they just went out there and did it. It's like if you tell someone they can't do something over and over again, they'll eventually believe you. But if never tell someone whether or not can do something, you simply just don't say anything, they'll probably believe they can do it, and be successful if they try. The level of intelligence is often a less than required component of success and money. The more important thing about succeeding is having grit. Having the ability to take a hit, get back up, and keep at it. The willingness to not give up. If you have that, even if you start off as less-than-intelligent, as you keep trying new things and never giving up, you'll develop into a stronger emotional and intelligent human being while retaining all the skills you developed when you didn't question whether or not you should or shouldn't have done something.

And lastly, if you feel unintelligent, and that holds you back, you need to stop thinking that way. There are a ton of ways to make money, you can't think that just because a person has a degree or street smarts that they are more capable of making money than you, that's simply not true. You can do as much as they can, you just need to put the effort into acquiring a skill or coming up with an idea. Intelligent people are often seen as intelligent only because they have worked hard to develop ______ skill.
