Deepak Mehta, “I hope to arrive to my death, late, in love, and a little drunk.”
Written Dec 18

Two of my favorite bands are Pink Floyd and Sigur Rós, and almost all of their mellower songs would, in my opinion, make for the perfect melodious companion during the transition from life to death.

However, if I had to pick just one song, it wouldn’t from either of the aforementioned. I would go with Hurt, the Johnny Cash version. There’s something about the lyrics, the haunting melody, and the fact that Cash died 7 months after the filming of the music video (which is every bit as evocative as the song itself).

The original writer, Trent Reznor, himself confessed after watching the video that “..that song isn’t mine anymore.”

Some other songs that would be worthy candidates for anyone’s swan song are:

#1: My Immortal - Evanescence

I wish that you would just leave
'Cause your presence still lingers here
And it won't leave me alone

These wounds won't seem to heal
This pain is just too real
There's just too much that time cannot erase

The song was written by the guitarist Ben Moody and is about the death of his grandfather. Amy Lee's voice lends it a surreal, magical quality.

#2: Adagio for Strings - Samuel Barber

#3: Hallelujah - Jeff Buckley

It's hard to argue that this might probably be the greatest cover of all time. Buckley nails it on the head with the most perfect rendition of a classic song ever.\

A song about pain and angst and about people "talking without speaking, hearing without listening"

Elizabeth Jeter, Published Author, Mental Health Advocate, Lover of Science
Written Dec 20

When I am in my final moments nearing my last breath, I want to have Nuvole Bianche by Ludovico Einaudi[1] playing on repeat until I am gone.

I first heard this song about a year ago when I was painting. It came on through my Pandora app and resonated with me in ways that I could not understand.

The beginning of the video has a quote that I was not aware of until researching it, just a few minutes ago.

"Regarde attentivement car ce que tu vas voir n’est plus ce que tu viens de voir"

Translation: “Look carefully; what you'll see is not what you've seen”

The song takes you through a slow yet steady beginning, when suddenly the pace is new, bright and expansive. As you progress, there are elements added into the music which bring unexpected beauty and curiosity. This curiosity speeds up into a more rapid tempo of uncertainty combined with peaks and valleys, highs and lows, joy and pain. The song eventually reverts to it’s beginning, where you find yourself with a calm understanding that despite the peaks and valleys, highs and lows, joy and pain…there is still beauty in uncertainty.

Footnotes

[1] Ludovico Einaudi - Nuvole Bianche [HD]

Mel Possehl, I've looked death in the eye more than once and lived to tell about it.
Written Dec 14

Hallelujah.

Without a doubt. And at one time, it would have been Rufus Wainwright’s version.

Until someone who knows me very, very well, sent me a link. And from that night years ago, and likely until the day I die, has and will be the ONE song, the ONE sound, that will ALWAYS be able to move something in me - even when all else fails.

The words…the piano…the violin…the voice…the passion with which it is sung. I could feel completely dead inside, as though there were nothing left of my soul and spirit, and this song could make me feel. Anger, pain, resentment…all churning into a spark that ignites a forward momentum to becoming stronger; becoming me…again.

When something has that much affect on you, it really does become a part of your soul. This is that something for me, and would be the last song I would want to hear before I do depart this earth. Because, God knows it almost has been already. Nonetheless, pain has one hell of a way of reminding you that you’re alive.

And who wouldn’t want to feel alive again one last time before they die?

Susan Bertolino, studied at University of Chicago
Written Feb 2

There is one song by Jim White I would like to hear. It is called Christmas Day.

It is about a man who is stuck at a bus station on Christmas Day, then he senses the presence of his lover, who is no longer in his life. The person may be deceased or simply has moved on.

Something about the song reminds me of my mother, who died suddenly and unexpectedly in May, 2000. Even now, I feel lost without her. My son grew up not knowing his grandmother. Aside from my husband and son, my mother was the key person in my life. Only one of my sisters comes close to filling a bit of that absence, and I know that she too, feels that great sadness from losing her as we did. I often think that my mother will meet me when I pass on. I can tell her how much I have missed her since her death. I wish I could tell her about watching my son grow up. She would have been so fond of him. Her death was as much his loss as it was to my siblings and me.

I often cry when I hear this song, because it reminds me of my grief. But if I were dying, I would see it as my way of finding her again. Even if there is no afterlife and I never see my mother again, I want to be thinking about her as I pass on to the next realm, whether it is dirt or another form of consciousness.
